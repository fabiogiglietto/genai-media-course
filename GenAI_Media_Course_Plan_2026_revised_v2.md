## Detailed Session Descriptions

### Week 1: Fundamentals of Generative AI (Feb 23–25)

**Mon Feb 23 | Introduction to Generative AI + This Week in AI**

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min): Recent developments. LECTURE: Course overview, definitions, historical evolution from early algorithms to LLMs. Mapping the generative AI landscape across modalities: text (LLMs), images (diffusion models), audio (voice cloning, AI music), and video (synthetic video generation). Introduction to the hybrid media system concept.

**Tue Feb 24 | The Hybrid Media System: Algorithms & Circulation on Meta and TikTok**

From broadcasting to hybrid media: platforms, algorithms, automation. Transformations in journalism and communication professions. Discussion of Chadwick's framework. FOCUS ON PLATFORM MECHANISMS: How recommendation algorithms shape content circulation on Meta (Facebook/Instagram) and TikTok. Viral dynamics, algorithmic amplification, network effects and echo chambers in the AI era. Comparative analysis of content moderation and AI-labeling policies across platforms.

**Wed Feb 25 | Workshop: Google Gemini & NotebookLM**

Hands-on with Google Gemini (text generation, multimodal, Imagen) and NotebookLM (document analysis, audio overviews). Students create a NotebookLM notebook with course readings.

### Week 2: Content Production, Disinformation & Regulation (Mar 2–4)

**Mon Mar 2 | Content Production with GenAI + This Week in AI**

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min): News and developments. LECTURE: Content creation workflows with Google Gemini, prompt engineering fundamentals. Introduction to Morosoli et al. (2025) on AI in journalism.

**Tue Mar 3 | GUEST: Vincenzo Cosenza — Augmented Intelligence**

Based on "Esercizi di Intelligenza Aumentata": 10 rules and practical exercises for collaborating with AI. From sporadic to systematic use.

**Wed Mar 4 | Deepfakes, Platform Policies & Regulation**

Deepfake technologies across modalities: video face-swaps, AI-generated voice cloning (audio deepfakes), and synthetic video generation. Case studies of audio deepfakes in political campaigns and scams. Vaccari & Chadwick (2020) on political trust. EU Code of Practice on Disinformation, AI Act, and platform content policies (Meta, TikTok).

### Week 3: Guest Seminar – GenAI in Political Communication (Mar 9–11)

**Mon Mar 9 | Guest Seminar Part 1 + This Week in AI**

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min). Bruna Paroni: Course pre-survey results (AI usage patterns, sentiment, risk/benefit perceptions, CPO vs CoDIC cohort differences). Research framing: 80,000+ news links from Brazilian elections (2018/2022), integrating LLMs into academic research.

**Tue Mar 10 | Guest Seminar Part 2: LLMs-in-the-loop Methodology**

Bruna Paroni: Deep dive into Marino & Giglietto (2024) methodology. The 4-step pipeline: data collection → binary classification (supervised, fine-tuned model) → embedding-based clustering → GPT-4-turbo labeling. Key distinctions: chatbot vs API (scale, reproducibility, traceability). Validation at two levels: cluster coherence (levels 0–3) and label fit (4 criteria × 3-point scale). The expertise paradox.

**Wed Mar 11 | Guest Seminar Part 3: In-Class Workshop**

Bruna Paroni: Students replicated a simplified LLMs-in-the-loop pipeline in groups using Google Gemini. Activity 1: Binary classification of 20 Italian news headlines (individual → group consensus → Gemini → prompt refinement → comparison). Activity 2: Clustering + labeling + validation of political headlines using the 4-criteria validation framework. See `docs/week3-seminar-recap.md` for full details, dataset, and prompts used.

### Week 4: AI Slop Seminar & Project Launch (Mar 16–18)

**Mon Mar 16 | GUEST: Massimo Terenzi — AI Slop & Synthetic Virality**

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min). SEMINAR: "From AI Slop to Strategic Virality" — Synthetic engagement bait on Facebook, affective manipulation tactics, algorithmic amplification. Terenzi & Giglietto research on Italian communities.

**Tue Mar 17 | Project Launch: Classifying AI Slop & Engagement Patterns** *(revised)*

PROJECT BRIEFING: The instructor presents a pre-curated research dataset from the Terenzi & Giglietto (2025) study: a CSV file with post metadata and engagement metrics (reactions, comments count, shares, views) plus a shared Google Drive folder containing ~300–400 AI-generated images from Meta Content Library. All groups receive the **same image subset** to enable inter-group reliability comparison. Session activities:

1. **Dataset exploration** (20 min) — Walk through the CSV structure, understand columns and engagement metrics, browse the image folder to get a sense of the content variety.
2. **Group formation** (15 min) — Groups of 4–6 students. Roles: coordinator, AI analyst (prompt design), validator (human coding), data analyst (engagement patterns), writer. Roles are flexible; everyone participates in all phases. Registration on Moodle by end of day.
3. **Research question development** (30 min) — Each group develops a specific *classification angle*. Just as in the Paroni seminar they operationalized "political," groups now operationalize their content categories. Example angles:
   - **By visual subject:** children, animals, landscapes, religious imagery, patriotic content, etc.
   - **By emotional appeal strategy:** nostalgia, cuteness, awe, fear, humor, etc.
   - **By generation technique characteristics:** photorealistic, stylized/painterly, hybrid photo-AI, obvious artifacts, etc.
   - **By apparent intent:** engagement bait, commercial promotion, political messaging, community building, etc.
4. **Group presentations** (15 min) — Each group briefly presents their RQ, classification scheme, and preliminary hypotheses about which categories might drive more engagement.

**Wed Mar 18 | Lab: Prompt Design & Pilot Classification** *(revised — formerly "Collecting Posts & Comments")*

Hands-on lab where groups design and test their classification prompts with Gemini. The data collection step is eliminated (instructor has provided the dataset). Session workflow:

1. **Prompt drafting** (20 min) — Each group writes a first-draft prompt for Gemini to classify images according to their scheme. Include: role assignment, category definitions with examples, output format specification (structured table/JSON).
2. **Pilot test** (30 min) — Upload 10–15 images to Gemini, run the classification prompt, examine results. Identify: ambiguous cases, missing categories, categories that are too broad or too narrow.
3. **Prompt refinement** (20 min) — Based on pilot results, improve the prompt using the iterative approach practiced in the Paroni workshop: add explicit examples, clarify boundary cases, specify output format more precisely.
4. **Codebook documentation** (10 min) — Document the classification scheme in a shared Google Sheet: category names, definitions, examples, and decision rules for borderline cases. This becomes the codebook for both AI and human classification.

By end of session, each group should have: a tested and refined prompt, a documented codebook, and a pilot classification of ~30–50 images.

### Week 5: AI-Assisted Analysis & Validation (Mar 23–25)

**Mon Mar 23 | Lab: Scaling Up AI-Assisted Classification + This Week in AI** *(revised)*

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min). Groups apply their refined prompts to classify the full image set (~300–400 images). Session covers:

1. **Batch classification with Gemini** — Using Gemini's long context window and multimodal capabilities to process images in batches. Strategies for maintaining consistency across batches. Students record Gemini's classification for each image in their shared Google Sheet.
2. **Cross-referencing with CSV data** — Joining Gemini's image classifications back to the engagement metrics in the original CSV. Each row now has: post metadata + engagement stats + AI-assigned content category.
3. **Preliminary patterns** — Quick descriptive exploration: how many images per category? Which categories have the highest/lowest mean engagement? Any obvious outliers?

**Tue Mar 24 | Validating AI Analysis: Human vs Machine** *(revised)*

LLMs-in-the-loop validation following Marino & Giglietto (2024). This session has two parts:

1. **Human coding** (40 min) — Each group member independently classifies a shared validation sample of **50 images** using the group's codebook (no Gemini, no discussion). Results recorded in individual columns of the shared sheet.
2. **Reliability calculation** (30 min) — Students compute:
   - **Human-to-human agreement:** Cohen's kappa between each pair of human coders within the group. Are the categories well-defined enough for humans to agree?
   - **Human-to-AI agreement:** Cohen's kappa between each human coder and Gemini's classification. Does the AI align with human judgment?
   - **Confusion matrix:** Where do humans and AI disagree? Which categories are most problematic?
3. **Iteration decision** (10 min) — Based on kappa values: if agreement is low, refine the codebook and/or prompt and reclassify. If agreement is acceptable, proceed to engagement analysis.

Additionally, if groups have performed clustering or labeling with Gemini (e.g., asking Gemini to group images into emergent clusters), they apply the Paroni validation framework: cluster coherence (levels 0–3) and label fit (4 criteria: thematic alignment, implications, coverage, contextual alignment × 3-point scale).

**Inter-group reliability dimension:** Since all groups classified the **same images**, a cross-group comparison is possible. Groups that used different classification schemes will naturally produce different categories — but for any shared dimension (e.g., multiple groups classified "children" content), inter-group agreement can be assessed. This becomes a discussion point for the final synthesis.

**Wed Mar 25 | Group Consultation & Engagement Analysis** *(revised)*

Individual group consultations with the instructor. Each group presents:
- Their validated classification results (kappa values, confusion matrix)
- Preliminary engagement analysis: do validated content categories predict different levels of engagement (reactions, shares, views)?
- Methodological challenges encountered and strategies adopted

Groups consolidate results and prepare for writing. The engagement analysis is the core analytical step: using the validated content categories (independent variable) to explain variation in engagement metrics (dependent variable) from the CSV. Groups may explore questions such as: Do images depicting children receive more reactions than landscapes? Does nostalgia-based content get shared more than humor? Is there a relationship between apparent generation quality and engagement?

### Week 6: Writing & Synthesis (Mar 30 – Apr 1)

**Mon Mar 30 | Writing Workshop + This Week in AI**

THIS WEEK IN AI (10 min). Structuring the final research paper. Using NotebookLM to synthesize findings and literature. Academic writing with AI assistance. The revised paper structure:

1. **Introduction** — From AI Slop as a phenomenon to the specific research question. Why does understanding AI-generated content classification and its engagement patterns matter?
2. **Literature review** — AI-generated content on social media, platform dynamics, LLMs-in-the-loop methodology.
3. **Method** — Description of the dataset (origin, scope, limitations), classification scheme and codebook, Gemini prompts (full text in appendix), human validation protocol, inter-coder reliability metrics, engagement analysis approach.
4. **Results** — Classification distribution, validation metrics (kappa), confusion matrix analysis, engagement patterns by content category.
5. **Discussion** — Interpretation of findings, connection to Terenzi & Giglietto's framework, methodological reflections (what the AI caught vs. missed, the expertise paradox), limitations (no comments, no original context, single platform), implications for platform policy.
6. **Appendix** — Full Gemini prompts, complete codebook, supplementary tables.

**Tue Mar 31 | Group Work: Paper Drafting**

In-class collaborative writing session. Peer review and feedback. Final methodology and theory review.

**Wed Apr 1 | Course Synthesis & Final Consultations**

Collective discussion on main findings. **Cross-group comparison:** since all groups classified the same images, the final synthesis explores how different classification schemes reveal different patterns in the same data. What does a "visual subject" lens show that an "emotional appeal" lens misses, and vice versa? Methodological reflections. Future perspectives for GenAI in media. Final project guidance.

## Group Project: AI Slop Classification & Engagement Analysis

### Project Overview (Revised)

Students work in groups of 4–6 to produce a research paper analyzing AI-generated content ("AI Slop") from Italian Facebook pages. The project uses a **pre-curated dataset** provided by the instructor, drawn from the Terenzi & Giglietto (2025) study. All groups work on the **same subset of ~300–400 images** with corresponding engagement metrics. Each group develops a distinct classification scheme, applies the LLMs-in-the-loop methodology using Google Gemini, validates results through systematic human coding, and analyzes whether content categories predict engagement patterns.

### Data Available to Students

| Data element | Source | Format | Access |
|---|---|---|---|
| Post metadata + engagement metrics | Meta Content Library (instructor download) | CSV | Shared Google Sheet |
| AI-generated images (~300–400) | Meta Content Library (instructor download) | Image files (JPG/PNG) | Shared Google Drive folder |
| Post text | Included in CSV | Text column | Shared Google Sheet |
| Engagement: reactions, shares, views | Included in CSV | Numeric columns | Shared Google Sheet |
| Comments count | Included in CSV | Numeric column | Shared Google Sheet |
| Individual comments | **Not available** | — | — |
| Original post context (on Facebook) | **Not available** (MCL internal URLs) | — | — |

### What Students Do NOT Have Access To

- TikTok Research API
- Individual comment text (only aggregate comment counts)
- Original posts in their Facebook context (the MCL export URLs are internal)

### Research Focus (Revised)

- Developing and operationalizing a classification scheme for AI-generated visual content
- Applying Gemini's multimodal capabilities for systematic image classification
- Validating AI classifications through parallel human coding (inter-coder reliability)
- Analyzing whether content categories predict different engagement patterns (reactions, shares, views)
- Comparing classification approaches across groups (same images, different schemes)
- Connecting findings to platform dynamics and the AI Slop ecosystem described by Terenzi & Giglietto

### Methodology: LLMs-in-the-Loop Pipeline (Revised)

The pipeline adapts the Marino & Giglietto (2024) framework and the Paroni workshop experience to multimodal content:

| Pipeline step | Paroni workshop (Week 3) | Project application (Weeks 4–6) |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Data** | 20 Italian news headlines (provided) | ~300–400 AI-generated images + CSV (provided by instructor) |
| **2. Classification** | Binary: political / non-political | Multi-category: per group's classification scheme |
| **3. Prompt design** | Structured prompt with role + examples | Multimodal prompt: role + category definitions + image input |
| **4. AI coding** | Gemini classifies headlines | Gemini classifies images using group's prompt |
| **5. Human coding** | Individual → group consensus | Each member codes 50-image validation sample independently |
| **6. Comparison** | Group vs. Gemini results | Cohen's kappa: human–human and human–AI |
| **7. Iteration** | Prompt refinement if discrepancies | Codebook + prompt refinement based on confusion matrix |
| **8. Analysis** | — | Engagement metrics (DV) by validated content category (IV) |

### Inter-Group Reliability Design

Since all groups classify the **same ~300–400 images**, the project design enables a unique cross-group analysis dimension:

- **Within-group reliability:** Each group computes human–human and human–AI kappa for their own classification scheme. This validates whether the scheme is operationalized clearly enough.
- **Cross-group comparison (final synthesis):** Different classification schemes applied to identical images reveal complementary patterns. The Apr 1 synthesis session compares what each lens reveals about the AI Slop ecosystem. This is not about "which group is right" — it's about how analytical choices shape findings.
